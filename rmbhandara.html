Welcome to Bhandara

Bhandara has a mixed economy with agriculture, industries and forest resources. Bhandara is known for its large production of rice. Tumsar, a tahsil town, is a noted rice market. Bhandara town is also known as "Brass City" owing to the presence of a large brass products industry.

The district is also known for the Ordnance Factory Bhandara of the Ordnance Factories Board, which manufactures products for the Indian Armed Forces. It is located on an estate which is commonly known as Jawaharnagar colony.

Ashok Leyland, a Hinduja Group Company, has a production facility at Gadegaon near Bhandara. Sunflag Iron Steel company and Shivmangal Ispat Pvt. Ltd. are other major industrial undertakings in the district.

Agriculture is the backbone of Bhandara’s economy. The region is known for the cultivation of crops such as rice, soybean, wheat, sugarcane, cotton, and pulses. The soil is fertile, and irrigation systems from rivers like Wainganga help support agriculture.

The district is blessed with forest cover, which supports a variety of activities, including timber production, collection of minor forest products, and honey production.

Bhandara is home to a mix of communities, including various tribal groups. The prominent tribes in the region include the Gonds, Mavchis, and Kolams. These tribes have a rich cultural heritage and their traditional dance, music, and festivals contribute to the vibrant culture of the district.

Ambhora Bridge

A picturesque nature, with a panoramic confluence of five rivers, dense forests and a temple of Lord Shiva on a hill, is found in Ambhora, located on the border of Nagpur and Bhandara districts. Ambhora is renowned as an important pilgrimage site in Vidarbha.

It is the confluence of five rivers, Wainganga, Kanhan, Aam, Murja and Kolhari, flowing between the Brahmagiri mountain ranges. A replica of the Golden Gate Bridge in Los Angeles, USA, has been built on the broad bed of this river.

This cable-stayed bridge has been built using German technology. A sky gallery has been built on this bridge. Also, a 40-foot-high lift has been installed to climb it. Standing on the gallery, one can see a panoramic view of the riverbed and the surrounding area.

Koka Wildlife Sanctuary

The park is situated barely 20 km from the district of Bhandara and close to Nagzira Wildlife Sanctuary. The total area of the park is 92.34 sq.

km. Koka has an abundant population of tigers and leopards. There are herbivores like Gaurs, Cheetals and Sambhars.

Koka plays the role of a safe haven for animals that wander away from Nagzira and New Nagzira Sanctuaries in search of a new habitat.

The jungle safaris operate from 6:30 to 10:30 in the morning and from 3:00 to 6.00 in the evening. The route is 44 km. long and takes about 3-4 hours to cover.

The trees in the forest are 100 year old. Migratory birds from Siberia visit the lake inside the sanctuary during winter season. There is an abundant population of Tigers, leopards, Sloth bears, gaurs, Chital, Sambhar deer and wild dogs in the sanctuary.[1] There is no village inside Koka sanctuary.

Ambagarh fort

Ambagarh fort is a 12th-century fort located in the present day Bhandara district of Maharashtra. The fort is surrounded by thick, dense forest and is closely connected to the freedom struggle of India.

The fort originally belonged to the Gondwana kings and then came under the possession of Raja Raghuji Bonsle. In 1818, the fort became witness to a significant insurrection known as the Lanji rebellion led by Chimna Patel, who was a close associate of Appa Sahib, the then-ruler of Nagpur.

Ambagarh Fort was a crucial strategic point that was controlled by the freedom fighters. They had extended a chain of posts from Ambagarh to Chandpur, Rampaili, and Sangarhi, which made it difficult for the British to launch an attack. To suppress the rebellion, the British first sent a detachment under Captain Gordon and captured the town of Kamtha on September 18th. Six days after the capture of Kamtha, a second detachment was sent under Major Wilson to capture the fort of Ambagarh. The fort was heavily garrisoned by around 500 revolutionaries. However, after a strategic military observation, Wilson captured the fort without any fighting after the garrison fled to a neighbouring hill.

Gosekhurd Dam

Indirasagar Dam, also known as Gosikhurd Project, was established with an aim of offering irrigation facilities to the villages of Nagpur, Bhandara and Chandrapur districts. The foundation of the dam was laid by Smt. Indira Gandhi, former Prime Minister of India on 23rd October 1984.

The site features 92 m high and 653 m long concrete gravity dam, which was formed after rehabilitating around 249 villages that were affected by the submergence of Gosikhurd. Presently, the dam fulfils the irrigation and electricity needs of the destination and its adjoining areas.

This dam is constructed across the Gose River, a tributary of the Wainganga River, which is a significant river in the region. The main purpose of the Gose Dam is to provide water for irrigation, helping to support the agricultural activities in the surrounding areas. It also helps in managing water resources for local villages and ensures a steady water supply during dry periods.

Rawanwadi Dam

Rawanwadi Dam was constructed as part of irrigation projects by Government of Maharashtra in the year 1960. It is built on and impounds a Local nallah.

This dam is primarily used for irrigation purposes, helping to supply water to the surrounding agricultural areas. It plays a crucial role in water management and supporting the local economy, particularly farming.

The dam is situated in a region with natural scenic beauty and is a part of Maharashtra's efforts to improve water availability in rural areas. It is built across a river or stream, like most small dams in the region, to manage the water resources for the agricultural needs of the population.
